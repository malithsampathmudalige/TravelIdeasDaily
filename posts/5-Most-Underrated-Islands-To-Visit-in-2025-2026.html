5 Most Underrated Islands To Visit in 2025/2026

Written by Elena

Published Dec 21, 2025

Everyone is going to Bali. Or Santorini. Or the Maldives. And that’s fine, if you like fighting for a sun lounger and paying triple for a cocktail. But the ocean is huge. There are thousands of rocks floating out there that offer the same white sand and blue water without the influencer crowds. As we head into 2025, the best travel isn't about hitting the "top 10" lists everyone else is reading. It's about finding spots that still feel real. Quiet. Cheaper. Places where the locals are actually happy to see you. Here are ten islands where you can still find a bit of adventure before the rest of the world catches on.

1. São Tomé and Príncipe, Africa

A quiet morning in Sorrento

This place is barely on the map for most people. Floating in the Gulf of Guinea, it’s a two-island nation that feels like Jurassic Park. We’re talking volcanic spikes, jungle that swallows everything, and beaches that look fake. It’s wild. The Portuguese influence is still there in the crumbling architecture, but the vibe is distinctly African. You don't come here for all-inclusive resorts. You come to hike through rainforests that haven't changed in centuries. Oh, and the chocolate? Incredible. They grow cacao everywhere. You can visit the plantations, see how it's made, and eat your weight in the dark stuff.

2. Flores, Indonesia

A quiet morning in Sorrento

Bali is a parking lot these days. Go east. Flores is long, rugged, and feels like the frontier. The main draw? Dragons. Actual dragons. The Komodo National Park is right next door, and staring down a Komodo dragon is something you don't forget. But stay for the rest of it. The diving is world-class—manta rays, sharks, pristine reefs. Inland, the Kelimutu volcano has three crater lakes that change color like mood rings depending on the season. It’s eerie and beautiful. The villages are traditional, the roads are winding, and the adventure is real

3. Vis, Croatia

A quiet morning in Sorrento

Croatia is booming, but Vis is chill. Why? Because it was a military base until 1989. Foreigners weren't allowed in. That isolation saved it. No massive hotel chains ruined the skyline. Instead, you get sleepy fishing villages, stone houses, and old guys drinking wine at 10 AM. It’s the Mediterranean from forty years ago. Rent a scooter and get lost. Visit the vineyards. The local white wine, Vugava, is dangerous in the best way. Nearby, the Blue Cave glows with this crazy neon light, but the real magic is just sitting at a waterfront café in Komiža, eating fresh lobster and doing absolutely nothing.

4. Palawan, Philippines

img src="../img/quezon-waterfalls-palawan.jpg" class="img-fluid" alt="Hidden rainforest waterfall with emerald pools in Quezon, Palawan, Philippines" caption="A hidden waterfall deep in Quezon’s rainforest showcases Palawan’s untouched natural beauty">

A quiet morning in Sorrento

Okay, Palawan isn't a secret. But the tiny islands around Coron and El Nido? That's where you go. These limestone cliffs shoot straight out of the turquoise water. It looks like a movie set. Skip the big group boat tours. Hire a local fisherman to take you out. You’ll find lagoons hidden behind rock walls and beaches where you’re the only person leaving footprints. Beneath the surface, there are Japanese shipwrecks from WWII. They’re shallow enough to snorkel over. Coral grows on the guns; fish swim through the portholes. It’s haunting and spectacular

5. Faroe Islands, Denmark

A quiet morning in Sorrento

Pack a raincoat. Seriously. Situated between Norway and Iceland, the Faroes are moody, wet, and wind-battered. They are also stunning. The cliffs are vertical walls of green grass dropping into the freezing Atlantic. Waterfalls don't just flow; the wind sometimes blows them back *up* the cliff. It feels like the edge of the world. The hiking is tough but worth it for the views. There are more sheep than people, and the roofs of the houses are made of grass. It’s cozy, though. The food is preserved, salty, and distinct, and the locals are tough but welcoming.
